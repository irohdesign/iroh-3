For Beginners

You should start with lighter strings. They are thinner, so they will be easier on your fingers. Light strings are especially good if you haven't built up callouses.

Light strings are easier to bend, allowing you to experiment easier than thicker strings do. They will be less painful and it will be easier to fret notes. Your fingers are less likely to become fatigued when using thinner strings, meaning you will be able to practice for longer periods of time.

Things to Consider

Thin strings do not have as much volume or sustain, when compared to thicker strings. They break more easily, so you might have to replace them more often. When purchasing thin strings, you should go for pure nickel, as they'll last longer than nickel-plated steel.

For Experts

You should experiment with heavier strings. Your hands will be able to tolerate the increased pressure, and so you're free to try out strings with warmer and darker tones.

Heavier strings offer more volume and sustain, hold tension better when you tune down, and can take more of a beating before breaking. Going from light to medium, or medium to heavy, will let you see how you like the darker tones.

Things to Consider

Heavier strings' thickness makes them harder to fret, bend and play. The strings will exert more tension on the neck, so you should be careful about putting them on a vintage and/or fragile guitar.

For Rock

You should try out both light and medium strings. Experiment with several string gauges and brands to find your own balance of the two.

Lighter strings are easier to bend notes and solo, but give up too much volume. Heavier strings have better sustain and volume, but are more difficult to fret. Experiment with these options and see where your middle-ground is.

Take into account your experience level and personal preferences. If you are less experienced, start with thinner strings.

For Metal

You should experiment with heavier strings. Your hands will need to tolerate the increased pressure.

Metal features a lot of dropped tuning, so heavier strings are a must. They will hold more tension on the guitar, stabilizing your dropped tunings. Heavier strings offer more volume and sustain, hold tension better when you tune down, and can take more of a beating before breaking.

Things to Consider

Heaver strings are most difficult to play, so expect an adjustment period. You may also want to spring for coated strings, as they last longer. Start with only prepackaged gauge variations, as they are the most balanced.
